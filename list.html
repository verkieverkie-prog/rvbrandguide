The Walk-Away Checklist: 12 checks before you hand over a dollar

One buyer put a deposit down, then saw daylight through the wall seams at the walkthrough. He ran these kinds of checks, and he got his money back. Run every one of them on any rig, from any dealer, before you sign anything.

Print this checklist

1. Pull the VIN on nhtsa.gov before you leave the lot

Open recalls on the exact unit, not the model line. There is no such thing as a minor recall on something you are about to finance for 15 years.

2. Hire an independent NRVIA certified inspector

Never rely on the selling dealer's inspection. They have a financial incentive to close the sale, not to protect you.

3. Press the walls and the floor

Spongy means water in the core. That is not a repair, that is a rebuild. Walk away.

4. Knock on the exterior wall

Hollow and thin is laminated stick-build. Solid and dense is fiberglass. Thirty seconds, no tools, and the wall cannot lie to you.

5. Demand the construction spec sheet in writing

Ask what the sidewall substrate is, what the framing is, and what the roof membrane is. If the answer is luan or oriented strand board, that wall is a countdown timer the first time a seam leaks.

6. Run every slide out under load, three times

Listen for binding, grinding, or motor strain. Slides are one of the most expensive things to fix and one of the easiest to test.

7. Inspect every seal and seam

Slide seals, roof seams, corner moldings. That is where the water always wins, and where the corners get cut.

8. Read the tire date codes, not the tread

Trailer tires age out at 5 to 7 years no matter how deep the tread looks, because they rot from the inside first.

9. Do the math on the weight placard

Check the cargo capacity against how you will actually load. And do not assume the sticker is right: a February 2025 federal recall covered 1,833 trailers because the weight rating on the placard itself was printed wrong.

10. Run the owner-forum test

Search the exact model and floor plan number plus the word "leak", then "slide", then "warranty". The same failure under three different owners is a pattern, not bad luck.

11. Demand a written pre-delivery inspection checklist from the dealer

If they cannot produce one, walk away. If they get defensive, run.

12. On repos and auction units: know what damage means

Cosmetic damage is negotiating leverage. Structural damage is a walk-away, at any price.
